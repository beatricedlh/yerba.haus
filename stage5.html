Once the mate is prepared, the gourd is passed from person to person in a circle. Each participant drinks the full infusion before returning it to be refilled. This cycle of sipping and passing continues until the yerba gradually loses its flavour. (Pite; Sarreal)

Sharing mate is organized through repetition rather than simultaneity. One person typically controls the pouring and passing, setting the pace of the ritual. This structure emphasizes turn-taking, attentiveness, and collective participation over individual consumption. Through repetition, mate reinforces social bonds and produces a sense of community grounded in shared practice. (Pite)

As yerba mate spread beyond its regions of origin, the practice of sharing adapted to new cultural contexts. Global trade and migration carried mate into new markets, where it was reinterpreted and sometimes individualized. The shared gourd now exists alongside packaged, portable alternatives shaped by global circulation. (Sarreal; Espinal)

Commercial production has transformed mate into a global commodity, influencing how it is marketed, consumed, and understood. Single-serve products and branded accessories prioritize convenience over communal ritual. These shifts reflect broader systems of globalization and labour, in which production, packaging, and distribution are increasingly shaped by international markets and workforces far removed from the act of sharing the gourd. (Espinal)
