Once the leaves are in place, hot water is added carefully to steep the yerba. The temperature is intentionally kept below boiling so the leaves infuse gradually rather than burn. This controlled pour allows flavour and compounds to be released slowly, supporting repeated refills from the same leaves. (Bae et al.; Gawron-Gzella et al.)

Water is added in stages rather than all at once, maintaining the structure of the yerba inside the gourd. Each pour extracts slightly different qualities from the leaves, making water management central to the practice of mate. The ritual depends on continuous access to hot water over time, not a single fixed serving. (Sarreal; Pite)

Despite this, at a broader scale, water is a finite resource shaped by geography and climate. Access to clean, reliable water depends on local ecosystems, infrastructure, and long-term environmental management. The act of steeping mate is therefore embedded in broader environmental systems that govern water availability. (Gawron-Gzella et al.)

Water use is closely tied to land use, particularly in agricultural regions where yerba mate is cultivated. Irrigation practices, deforestation, and monoculture farming can affect watersheds and soil health. The water poured into the gourd reflects upstream decisions about how land and resources are managed. (Sarreal; Bae et al.)

Moreover, access to water is not equally distributed and is often shaped by political and legal systems. In many parts of South America, water rights are regulated by state policies that determine who can use water, for what purposes, and at what cost. Everyday practices like preparing mate rely on these invisible governance structures. (Pite; Gawron-Gzella et al.)
