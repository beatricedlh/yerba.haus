The leaves placed into the gourd come from Ilex paraguariensis, a plant cultivated specifically for repeated infusion. Before reaching the consumer, these leaves pass through cycles of planting, growth, harvest, drying, aging, and grinding. What appears as a simple ingredient is the outcome of long-term agricultural processes. (Sarreal; Bae et al.)

Yerba mate cultivation depends on specific ecological conditions, including subtropical climates, consistent rainfall, and nutrient-rich soil. The plant grows slowly and requires years before it can be harvested, tying production to long-term land management. Climate variability directly affects yield, quality, and availability of yerba mate. (Bae et al.; Espinal)

Changes in temperature, rainfall patterns, and soil health influence how yerba mate is grown today. Increased demand places pressure on ecosystems as producers balance productivity with sustainability. The leaves in the gourd are therefore shaped by ongoing environmental conditions beyond the moment of consumption. (Bae et al.; Espinal)

Yerba mate production remains labour-intensive, involving planting, pruning, harvesting, and processing by hand in many regions. Workers play a central role in maintaining plantations and preparing leaves for market, yet their labour is often invisible in the final product. The act of filling the gourd relies on these human systems of work and expertise. (Pite)

As yerba mate moved from local consumption to regional and global trade, cultivation shifted toward larger-scale agricultural systems. Market demand influences how leaves are processed, blended, and distributed. Economic forces shape which leaves are grown, how they are harvested, and where they ultimately travel. (Sarreal; Espinal)
