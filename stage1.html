To begin, the gourd and bombilla are cleaned, arranged, and prepared. This step sets up the objects required for drinking mate and ensures they are ready for use.

stage 1

Mate is prepared using a small set of tools: a gourd, a metal straw known as a bombilla, and a vessel for hot water. These tools shape how the drink is consumed, allowing the leaves to be steeped repeatedly and filtered while drinking. Unlike single-use cups, this setup is designed for continuous refilling and shared use, making the tools central to the practice itself rather than interchangeable containers. (Pite)

The gourd and bombilla are not decorative additions but functional objects refined through long-term use. The bombilla’s filter prevents leaf particles from being swallowed, while the gourd’s size and shape support repeated pours without disturbing the yerba. Over time, these tools became standardized through practice, not formal design processes, responding directly to how mate is prepared and consumed. (Sarreal)

Before European contact, Indigenous Guaraní communities prepared and consumed yerba mate using natural containers and straws made from plant materials. These tools were embedded in systems of ecological knowledge, understanding local plants, materials, and methods of preparation. The foundational form of the mate toolkit emerged from Indigenous practices long before commercialization or global circulation. (Sarreal; Pite)

During the colonial period, mate tools began to change as European materials and aesthetics were introduced. Silver bombillas and decorated gourds became common, especially among colonial elites, transforming everyday objects into markers of status. While the ritual remained rooted in Indigenous practice, its tools were increasingly reshaped through colonial economies and social hierarchies. (Sarreal)

Today, the gourd and bombilla continue to carry layered meanings shaped by Indigenous knowledge, colonial history, and modern production. Whether handmade, inherited, or mass-produced, these tools signal continuity with past practices while adapting to contemporary contexts. Their persistence highlights how material objects can hold cultural memory while remaining active in everyday life.

Pite, Rebekah E. Sharing Yerba Mate: How South America’s Most Popular Drink Connects People, Power, and Place. University of North Carolina Press, 2023.

Sarreal, Julia J. S. Yerba Mate: The Drink That Shaped a Nation. University of California Press, 2023.
